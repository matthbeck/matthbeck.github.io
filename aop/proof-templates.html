  • \(\clubsuit\) or \(\heartsuit\).

    Proof: If \(\clubsuit\) is true, we are done.

    So now assume that \(\clubsuit\) is not true.

    [lots of good math]

    Thus \(\heartsuit\) is true.

  • There exists a unique \(x \in \clubsuit\) such that \(\heartsuit\).

    Proof:

    Existence: [show that there exists \(x \in \clubsuit\) such that \(\heartsuit\)]

    Uniqueness: Assume \(x_1, x_2 \in \clubsuit\) both satisfy \(\heartsuit\).

    [lots of good math]

    Thus \(x_1 = x_2\).

  • Proof of \(P(k)\) by induction:

    Base case: [prove that \(P(1)\) is true.]

    Induction step: Let \(n \in \mathbf{N}\); assume that \(P(n)\) is true.

    [lots of good math, using the assumption that \(P(n)\) is true]

    Thus \(P(n+1)\) is also true.

  • Proof of \(P(k)\) by strong induction:

    Base case: [prove that \(P(1)\) is true.]

    Induction step: Let \(n \in \mathbf{N}\); assume that \(P(j)\) is true for \(1 \le j \le n\).

    [lots of good math, using the assumption that \(P(1), P(2), \dots, P(n)\) are true]

    Thus \(P(n+1)\) is also true.

  • \(A=B\) (set equality).

    \(\subseteq\): Assume \(x \in A\).

    [lots of good math]

    Thus \(x \in B\).

    \(\supseteq\): Assume \(x \in B\).

    [lots of good math]

    Thus \(x \in A\).

  • \(f: A \to B\) is injective.

    Assume \(a_1, a_2 \in A\) with \(f(a_1) = f(a_2)\).

    [lots of good math]

    Thus \(a_1 = a_2\).

  • \(f: A \to B\) is surjective.

    Assume \(b \in B\).

    [lots of good math, finding an \(a \in A\) or proving its existence]

    Thus \(f(a) = b\).
